t = 47.44, p-value = 2.2*10^(-16) Since our p-value is significant, we reject the Null Hypothesis. That is, there is a difference in the mean hourly earnings between individuals who have at least a bachelors degree and those who do not. Critical value for this test is set at 0.005, therefore we can be sure that our findings are correct with 95% confidence.

When viewing the boxplot, the median hourly earnings for those with at least a bachelors to be higher than those without a bachelors. Additionally, when comparing the middle 50% of hourly earning for both groups, there is a lot more variation with hourly earnings for those with bachelors degrees than those without a bachelors degrees. Symmetry for both box plots was not even, where the distance between the top 25% of earners from the median was larger than the distance between the lowest 25% and the median. Hence, both groups were skewed noticeably to the right.

The line defining the upper quartile for the group with at least a bachelors degree was higher than the line defining the upper quartile for the group with no bachelors degree. The same could be said about the line defining the lower quartile for both groups. What this tells us is that overall individuals typically are earning more if they hold at least a bachelors degree.
